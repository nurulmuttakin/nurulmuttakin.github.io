Md Nurul Muttakin

Xu Lab, SCS, Carnegie Mellon University

Prof. Min Xu and Dr. Xueying Zhan
Scientifically Realistic 3D Cryo-ET Image Generation

The lack of supervisory information on 3D representations, especially in Cryo-ET, is pressing. Indeed, the unavailability of labeled data pervades bioscience. Thus, there is an urgent need to exploit unlabeled data in the 3D medical vision domain and generate synthetic realistic 3D images with pre-specified labels. To this end, we propose to use 3D conditional GANs to generate scientifically realistic subtomograms (3D Cryo-ET images). From our recent experiments, we have realized that evaluating the generative models for 3D Cryo-ET data properly is also a critical research question to be pursued. Thus we have two goals in this project: 1) design proper evaluation pipeline for 3D Cryo-ET data, and 2) generate scientifically realistic 3D Cryo-ET images to better solve supervised ML tasks in the Cryo-ET domain and healthcare.

Protein Function Prediction using Neuro-symbolic Learning

Proteins have over 50,000 functions. These functions are represented in a hierarchical gene ontology (GO), where complex neuro-symbolic axiomatic relationships exist. However, most existing PFP methods consider these functions independently. Some works attempt to exploit the background knowledge in GO with empirical intuition with no theoretical foundation. In addition, protein functions are only positively labeled, whereas experimental labels are available for only 0.025% (70K among 251M) of proteins. Therefore, it is imperative to exploit these neuro-symbolic relationships by incorporating the incompleteness of supervisory information. We plan to design a neuro-symbolic positive unlabeled contrastive learning method to generate embeddings for proteins, while preserving the neuro-symbolic relationships among different functions using the semantic similarity of GO classes. Sequence and structure based similarity scores will be used as class priors for positive unlabeled learning part. The outcome and insights of this project directly drive the herbicide resistant crop design and nanobody design projects at KAUST.

Overlapping Community Detection in Complex Edge-Attributed Weighted Protein-Protein Interaction Networks

Overlapping community detection is a crucial problem in many areas of science and engineering, including bioscience. The problem becomes even more intricate when considering a network with a complex set of heterogeneous attributes in the nodes and edges. Most existing overlapping community detection methods consider networks with only nodes and edges without any node and edge attributes. Very few works incorporate edge-weight information with little efficacy in community detection performance. Indeed, no overlapping community detection method can effectively incorporate a complex set of heterogeneous edge and node attributes to boost community detection performance. Though these attributes have critical information about a network’s overlapping community structure, existing methods are far from exploiting this substantial information. In our research, we aim to develop a novel overlapping community detection method that can exploit all available information in the network's edge attributes or node attributes using graph neural networks. As an application, we will detect overlapping communities in a protein-protein interaction network with a complex set of edge and node attributes. The detected overlapping communities in the PPI network should help to elucidate biological processes from the PPI network essentially discovering disease mechanisms.

MSc Thesis, BORG, KAUST

Prof. Robert Hoehndorf
Learning 3D structures for protein function prediction

Machine learning models such as AlphaFold can generate protein 3D conformation from primary sequence up to experimental accuracy, which gives rise to a bunch of research works to predict protein functions from 3D structures. Almost all of these works attempted to use graph neural networks (GNN) to learn 3D structures of proteins from 2D contact maps/graphs. Most of these works use rich 1D features such as ESM and LSTM embedding in addition to the contact graph. These rich 1D features essentially obfuscate the learning capability of GNNs. In this thesis, we evaluate the learning capabilities of GCNs from contact map graphs in the existing framework, where we attempt to incorporate distance information for better predictive performance. We found that GCNs fall far short with 1D-CNN without language models, even with distance information. Consequently, we further investigate the capabilities of GCNs to distinguish subgraph patterns corresponding to the InterPro domains. We found that GCNs perform better than highly rich sequence embedding with MLP in recognizing the structural patterns. Finally, we investigate the capability of GCNs to predict GO-terms (functions) individually. We found that GCNs perform almost on par in identifying GO-terms in the presence of only hard positive and hard negative examples. We also identified some GO-terms indistinguishable by GCNs and ESM2-based MLP models. This gives rise to new research questions to be investigated by future works.

Stylized Projected GANs for Realistic Image Generation Faster

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) generate novel data using a generator and a discriminator. GANs usually produce high-quality images, but training GANs in an adversarial setting is a difficult task because it requires high computation power and hyper-parameter regularization for converging. ProjectedGANs tackle the training difficulty by using transfer learning to project the generated and real samples into a pre-trained feature space. ProjectedGANs speed up the training time and convergence. However, it produces artifacts in the generated images, which reduce the quality of the generated samples. To overcome this limitation, we propose an optimized architecture called Stylized ProjectedGAN (SPGAN), which integrates the mapping network of the StyleGAN with the Skip Layer Excitation of FastGAN architecture. The integrated modules are incorporated within the generator architecture of the FastGAN to mitigate the problem of artifacts in the generated images.

MSc Thesis, GDIVL, BUET

Prof. Md. Saidur Rahman and Dr. Md. Iqbal Hossain
Overlapping Community Detection using Deep GCNs

It is a regrettable fact that the number of road traffic accidents continues to rise, largely due to rapid urban growth and the ever-increasing density of vehicles in cities and surrounding areas.However, with emerging sensor devices and the IoT, it has become feasible to configure future vehicles with safety sensors to prevent many of these accidents. Urban traffic forecasting models generally follow either a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) or a Support Vector Classifier (SVC) to estimate the features of potential road accidents. Although SVC can provide good performances with less data than GMM, it incurs a higher computational cost. In this project, we propose a novel framework that combines the descriptive strength of the Gaussian Mixture Model with the high-performance classification capabilities of the Support Vector Classifier. Experimental results show that the approach compares very favorably with baseline statistical methods.
